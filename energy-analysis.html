Project Overview

Large-scale renewable energy projects demand far more than thermodynamic analysis. This project combined geological resource assessment, technology selection, technoeconomic modeling, revenue optimization, environmental impact analysis, permitting strategy, transmission infrastructure planning, and stakeholder engagement to evaluate whether a utility-scale geothermal facility could be successfully deployed in northeastern Nevada.

The Challenge

Determine whether a blind stratigraphic geothermal resource in Mary's River Basin could support a commercially viable 30 MW binary-cycle power plant while navigating geological uncertainty, regulatory complexity, and community concerns.

The Solution

Develop a systems-level engineering framework that integrates resource characterization, economic analysis, grid interconnection, environmental stewardship, and long-term operational strategy to identify technical opportunities and project risks.

“Engineering decisions rarely depend on a single equation. Successful infrastructure projects emerge from balancing technical performance, economics, uncertainty, regulation, environmental stewardship, and human relationships.”

Engineering Decisions

Key Design Decisions

Why Binary Cycle Technology?

Binary cycle plants offer closed-loop operation, high reliability, and compatibility with moderate-temperature resources. Their ability to provide continuous power with minimal emissions made them an attractive choice for Mary's River Basin.

Why Mary's River Basin?

Previous geologic investigations and favorable heat flow data suggested significant untapped geothermal potential while maintaining proximity to existing transmission infrastructure.

Why Continuous Production?

Revenue analysis demonstrated that baseload operation outperformed peaking strategies, maximizing profitability and reducing operational complexity.

Why Systems-Level Analysis?

Technical performance alone does not determine project success. Permitting, stakeholder relationships, financing, and environmental constraints ultimately shape whether infrastructure can be built.

Engineering Challenges

Major Technical Risks

Large-scale energy projects are fundamentally exercises in uncertainty management. Throughout this project, I identified and analyzed the risks that would ultimately determine whether the plant could transition from concept to operation.

01

Reservoir Uncertainty

Blind stratigraphic systems present substantial subsurface uncertainty. Resource confirmation drilling represents one of the highest-risk investments because unfavorable results could render the entire project uneconomic.

02

Thermal Decline

Long-term reservoir sustainability depends on reinjection strategies and thermal breakthrough management to maintain output over decades of operation.

03

Grid Interconnection

Transmission infrastructure and utility approval timelines become critical-path items that strongly influence deployment schedules.

04

Community Acceptance

Public perception, tribal consultation, and land-use concerns can create project delays even when technical performance is strong.

05

Permitting Complexity

Federal, state, county, utility, and tribal stakeholders all influence project execution, creating overlapping regulatory requirements.

06

Financial Risk

Large capital requirements and extended development timelines demand robust economic analysis before investment decisions can be justified.

Technoeconomic Analysis

Balancing Engineering and Economics

One of the most interesting aspects of this project was recognizing that engineering optimization does not necessarily maximize economic performance.

I evaluated capital expenditures, operating costs, revenue streams, electricity market dynamics, and plant utilization strategies to determine how technical decisions influence profitability.

This process highlighted the importance of considering engineering systems through both physical and financial lenses.

Regulatory Strategy

More Than Just Engineering

Federal Agencies

Development required understanding permitting pathways involving BLM, DOE, NEPA reviews, environmental assessments, and geothermal leasing processes.

State-Level Requirements

Nevada regulations surrounding drilling permits, water resources, and environmental protection created additional constraints on project execution.

Utility Interconnection

Transmission studies and agreements with NV Energy introduced schedule uncertainty and infrastructure considerations.

Local Stakeholders

County governments, ranchers, wildlife groups, and nearby residents all influence whether a project gains social acceptance.

Community Outreach

Building Social License

Engineering Success Depends on People

Technical feasibility alone does not guarantee project success. Effective communication, trust, transparency, and stakeholder engagement are equally important components of infrastructure deployment.

Ranching Communities

Developed strategies focused on minimizing land-use disruptions and maintaining productive relationships with local agricultural stakeholders.

Tribal Consultation

Recognized the importance of engaging with the Te-Moak Tribe and respecting the cultural significance of Western Shoshone lands.

Wildlife Conservation

Considered habitat preservation and mitigation measures for protected species throughout project development.

Public Education

Identified opportunities to address misinformation and improve public understanding of geothermal energy technologies.

Thinking Like an Owner

Engineering Through the Lens of Project Risk

This project changed the way I think about engineering. Instead of asking "Can this system work?", I began asking "Should this system be built?", "Who does it affect?", and "What risks determine success or failure?" Those questions ultimately proved more valuable than any individual calculation.
