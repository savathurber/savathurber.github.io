Biodegradable Gas Generation System for Autonomous Marine Propulsion

Designed and experimentally validated a passive oxygen-generation system capable of delivering pressurized gas for propulsion while maintaining biodegradability and eliminating mechanical actuation.

View Final Prototype Back to Projects

Project Overview

Problem

Traditional power systems are difficult to integrate into biodegradable marine robots. The challenge was to generate pressurized gas in a fully passive and environmentally compatible manner for underwater propulsion.

Solution

Using catalytic decomposition of hydrogen peroxide with yeast, our team designed and tested several controlled-release mechanisms before demonstrating successful propulsion system integration.

Engineering Contributions

Concept Generation

Generated and screened multiple controlled-release architectures using morphological analysis and iterative design.

Prototype Development

Designed vessel concepts and fabricated numerous proof-of-concept mechanisms.

Experimental Testing

Performed pressure generation, flow characterization, leak testing, and subsystem integration experiments.

Failure Analysis

Investigated clogging, foaming, residue accumulation, sealing failures, and reaction control challenges.

Design Evolution

DBT 1 — Chemistry Validation

Verified that hydrogen peroxide and yeast could produce useful pressures.

DBT 2 — Capsule Mechanisms

Tested staged delivery concepts using gelatin capsules.

DBT 3 — Funnel and Spiral Designs

Investigated controlled-release geometries and flow behavior.

DBT 4 — Washer Stack Mechanism

Developed a weighted water-soluble paper system with improved control.

Final Integration Testing

Successfully powered the propulsion team's system.

Technical Challenges

Reaction Control

Bulk mixing produced excessive foam and unstable gas production.

Clogging

Yeast residue frequently blocked valves and tubing.

Sealing

Pressure leaks prevented reliable measurements and system operation.

Gas Delivery

Flow stability became equally important as pressure generation.

Final Prototype

The final washer-stack design used water-soluble paper and weighted structures to increase reaction surface area, reduce clogging, and maintain passive operation.
